We all face uncertainty at some point in our lives. Whether it comes from disruption or from stretching into something new, uncertainty can hit without notice or grace. During these bouts, it can be hard to focus or be productive.

Two of the most common forces I've seen cause uncertainty:

1. When you stretch yourself to try something new in uncharted territory
2. When the way you've defined yourself is disrupted—through a relationship, a job, a status, or something else

## A Story About Layoffs and Discovery

Let me tell you about a time when disruption was high and uncertainty was ripe. I once endured three rounds of layoffs within an 18-month period. That first round was a gut punch—losing friends and colleagues, with uncertainty like a visible thick fog in the air you couldn't escape.

I stuck it out and did what I now call "map who you are before deciding who you become next." There's too much to tell on that topic (which is why there will be a book on it), but a small sample is this: I discovered that I love coming up with ideas and want to be in the room for that. I do not excel at—or enjoy as much—executing a hundred steps from point A to point B. Product Management became a natural path because I get to ideate more than I operate.

That uncertainty eventually led to one of my biggest career moves, breaking into Product Management. I've now had the pleasure of working in that space for six years.

## Uncertainty in 2026

In 2026, uncertainty has arrived again—this time knocking from both doors.

I've been working on Modern Compass for a year and a half. I have a completed manuscript that I'm editing now (and behind, frankly, because I have to execute those hundred steps). The uncertainty comes from second-guessing myself now that beyond editing there is copy editing. For the most part, beyond these edits, this is what the world will see—however small or large that ends up being—and I haven't done any of this before. The second door is career uncertainty again, this time for different reasons I won't go into here.

So what do we do? Do we just live with it and let it ruminate?

Honestly, a little yes. I think there is a period where you let uncertainty run its course—similar to the way we're told to let grief in to process it. Let it show you what it believes is the worst possible outcome. Once you spot it, it's time to move away from it.

Here's how I'd propose moving through uncertainty using the Modern Compass directions.

## Self

Self-worth isn't earned the way confidence is—in fact, it's not earned at all. It's a universal truth that we all have worth. The work lies in finding ways to reconnect with that truth if we've lost our connection to it. The sooner you understand the delineation between Worth and Confidence, the sooner you will protect your sense of Worth when uncertainty arrives.

## Trust

Uncertainty has a way of exposing where you've been placing trust you shouldn't have, and withholding it from people who've earned it. Take an honest look at both. Where have you been leaning on institutions, relationships, or narratives that haven't held up? Where have you been reluctant to trust someone who has consistently shown up for you? Redirect accordingly.

## Relationships

Take stock of the depth of your various relationships. If uncertainty is running rampant and you haven't dedicated consistent time to relationships—personal or professional—start now. You're operating at a serious disadvantage without them. One of the biggest lessons I've learned: connecting is just as important as accomplishments. Accomplishments are a one-time benefit. Connections can offer a recurring one.

## Character

This one is the simplest yet most telling of all four. Character is what you display to the world through your actions, effort, and lived experiences. Uncertainty reveals your true colors, your fight-or-flight instincts, and your determination. Reflect on how you show up when faced with uncertainty—you will often find common themes in how you respond. In times of uncertainty, the people around you see your character more clearly than ever.
